Visual Pathways Case Study

The Challenge

In today's job market, a staggering 70% of American workers find themselves unsatisfied with their career choices, a condition that emphasises an issue of career misalignment and dissatisfaction. The modern professional is expected to change careers an average of 5-7 times during their working life, showing a significant gap in career exploration and decision-making processes.

Problem Statement

How can we design an immersive app that addresses widespread career dissatisfaction by offering interactive, VR-based simulations of various professions, guided by industry professionals, to provide users with a comprehensive and engaging way to explore potential career paths, better aligning their skills and interests with real-world job expectations?

Solution

Visual Pathways directly tackles the challenge of career dissatisfaction through an immersive VR platform that builds upon conventional career guidance methods. By simulating real-life work experiences across various industries, it allows students to explore and understand different career paths through virtual reality. This innovative approach helps to demystify job roles and spark interest in various fields by offering students a unique look at potential careers guided by an industry professional. It makes career exploration interactive and engaging, enabling students to better understand their preferences and make informed decisions about their future, minimizing future career changes and dissatisfaction.

User Flow

Prototyping

First Iteration Prototyping

  • Students filled out our personality quiz.
  • Students then watched their top 3 matches’ VR demo videos independently (sourced from Transfr).
  • Students joined Slack to keep in touch with their recruiters.
  • Students then decided on their favorite based on the VR demo and went to a table to speak with a recruiter and complete “aptitude tests”.
In-person Feedback Survey Feedback IA Feedback
  • Aptitude tests didn’t translate very well.
  • Too many people chose popular careers, making it crowded and hard to speak to the recruiter.
  • No incentive to use Slack.
  • People did not find the careers relevant.
  • Career quiz was easy to take.
  • It was interesting to watch the VR video and see what the career might look like.
  • Individual VR sessions were not interactive between student and recruiter.
  • Unclear why recruiters would benefit from using our product.
  • Networking and VR experience were disjointed experiences.

Incorporating Feedback

Second Iteration Prototyping

Career options not relevant VR experience lacked interaction Make product more beneficial for recruiters

In Prototype 1, we were limited to careers that already had VR demo videos. For Prototype 2, we changed the careers to be more relevant because we no longer prototyped the VR experience as a video, but rather as an interaction.

Our first prototype had the VR demos as an independent task. This excluded recruiters from being able to interact with the students as they went along the demo. For our second prototype, we had students and recruiters enter the VR environment together to complete the aptitude tasks.

Our first prototype was not obviously beneficial to the recruiters. For our second prototype, we had recruiters using VR to complete the aptitude tasks and also had the recruiters select their standout students at the end. This enables recruiters to actually find people interested in the field and showing early talent.

Prototyping

  • Students filled out the career matching quiz once again.
  • After finding out their top 2 matches, they joined a Slack channel where they were able to introduce themselves to their fellow peers.
  • We then had them go to their 1st match’s table to do the VR walkthrough, which was essentially an aptitude test where the student and recruiter could converse about the job.
  • After 5 minutes, they did a rotation of the same thing with their 2nd match.
  • After this process, the “recruiter” reached out to the top candidate from their session to show interest in furthering the network connection.

Reflection

Visual Pathways was an ambitious project that sought to address a critical issue of career dissatisfaction by providing users with immersive, VR-based simulations of various professions. Throughout this journey, I played a multifaceted role as a UX Designer, UX Researcher, and Tester, contributing to the project's success over a period of 10 weeks.

Key Learnings and Experiences

User-Centric Design - One of the most rewarding aspects of this project was the deep dive into user research. Engaging with students, understanding their frustrations and needs, and translating those insights into actionable design elements was both challenging and enlightening. It reaffirmed the importance of a user-centric approach in designing impactful solutions.

Collaboration and Communication - Working closely with industry professionals and team members across different disciplines underscored the value of effective communication and collaboration. Utilizing tools like Figma for design and Slack for team communication helped streamline our workflow and ensured that everyone was aligned with the project goals.

Innovation and Adaptability - The innovative nature of using VR for career exploration required us to constantly adapt and think outside the box. It was a valuable exercise in flexibility and problem-solving, pushing the boundaries of conventional career guidance methods and leveraging technology to create a more engaging and interactive user experience.

Testing and Feedback - Conducting usability tests and gathering feedback from our target audience was crucial in refining our design. This iterative process of testing and improving highlighted the importance of validating design assumptions and being receptive to user feedback to enhance the overall product experience.

Challenges and Solutions

Technical Constraints - Integrating VR technology posed several technical challenges, from ensuring compatibility with various devices to creating realistic and immersive simulations. Collaborating with technical experts and continuously testing the VR functionalities helped overcome these hurdles.

User Engagement - Maintaining user engagement in a VR environment required careful consideration of interaction design principles. We focused on creating intuitive and user-friendly interfaces, as well as providing clear guidance and feedback within the simulations to keep users immersed and engaged.

Time Management - Managing a project of this scale within a 10-week timeframe was demanding. Prioritizing tasks, setting realistic milestones, and maintaining a structured project timeline were essential in ensuring timely delivery of the project.

Impact and Future Directions

The Visual Pathways project has the potential to revolutionize career exploration by providing users with a realistic and immersive way to experience different professions. By offering a deeper understanding of various career paths, it empowers users to make more informed decisions about their futures, potentially reducing career dissatisfaction and turnover.

Moving forward, there are several areas for future development and improvement:

  • Enhanced VR Experiences: Continuously improving the realism and interactivity of the VR simulations to provide an even more immersive experience.
  • Broader Reach: Expanding the range of professions and industries covered by the platform to cater to a wider audience.
  • Longitudinal Studies: Conducting longitudinal studies to measure the long-term impact of the VR career simulations on users' career satisfaction and success.

In conclusion, working on Visual Pathways was a profoundly enriching experience that underscored the power of user-centric design and innovative technology in addressing real-world problems. It has strengthened my skills as a UX Designer and Researcher, and I look forward to applying these learnings to future projects aimed at creating positive social impact.
